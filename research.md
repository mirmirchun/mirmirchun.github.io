Many existing AI evaluations focus on model-centered performance, such as capability, accuracy, reasoning, knowledge, and safety violations. These measures are important, but they do not fully answer a critical question for real-world capacity-building contexts: **Do AI interactions strengthen or undermine the agency, judgment, and capacity of the people using them?**

My doctoral research responds to this gap by developing an **empowerment-centered approach to AI evaluation**. I study whether LLM interactions can support empowerment by helping users understand their situation and constraints, build on local strengths, navigate available resources, and make decisions that can be translated into locally relevant action.

This research is grounded in **social work theory, empowerment theory, the Capability Approach, AI evaluation, and systems-level capacity building**.

## Core Research Question

Can LLM interactions support empowering capacity building in resource-constrained settings?

I define empowering interaction as interaction that helps users:

* understand their situation, choices, and structural constraints;
* recognize their own strengths and surrounding resources;
* explore possible support systems and referral pathways;
* strengthen contextual judgment;
* make self-directed decisions that can be translated into action.

In this work, resource-constrained capacity-building settings refer to contexts where individuals, organizations, or communities are trying to build capacity but face limited access to specialized expertise, formal training, human resources, financial and material resources, institutional support, referral systems, decision-making authority, and locally relevant knowledge.

## Current Research Aims

### 1. Develop an empowerment-centered evaluation framework

I am developing criteria to evaluate whether LLM interactions support empowering processes, including informed choice, agency, strengths-based reasoning, resource navigation, contextual responsiveness, and sustainable capacity building.

### 2. Design benchmark tasks for capacity-building contexts

I am creating benchmark tasks that reflect realistic capacity-building challenges in resource-constrained settings. These tasks are designed to evaluate whether LLM responses simply provide information or help users reason through complex situations, constraints, and possible actions.

### 3. Evaluate LLM responses using empowerment criteria

Using the framework and benchmark tasks, I evaluate LLM responses to examine whether they support or undermine empowerment. This includes assessing whether responses are context-sensitive, non-paternalistic, strengths-based, resource-aware, and useful for practical decision-making.

### 4. Apply LLM-based case learning in PRM education

I apply this research to **Physical and Rehabilitation Medicine (PRM)** education in Sub-Saharan Africa, where formal specialty training, teaching faculty, clinical resources, and locally relevant learning materials may be limited.

The study uses LLM-based case learning with local physicians and health providers to examine whether AI can support learning and capacity building in realistic educational settings.

### 5. Conduct field evaluation with local health providers

I work with local health providers to assess whether LLM-based case learning is experienced as empowering, contextually relevant, and useful for strengthening practical capacity. This field evaluation is central to the research because empowerment cannot be measured only by model output; it must also be examined through the experience of the people using the system.

## Why This Matters

AI tools are increasingly being used in education, health, development, and social support contexts. In resource-constrained settings, LLMs may become important learning supports where access to formal expertise and training infrastructure is limited.

However, AI should not be evaluated only by whether it produces correct answers. It should also be evaluated by whether it strengthens human agency, supports contextual judgment, builds on local strengths, and contributes to sustainable capacity.

My research aims to contribute an evaluation approach that connects AI research with social work values and global health equity.
